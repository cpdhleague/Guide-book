Since building this deck, I've learned this curve archetype now has a name: the 2-4-7 curve. The concept is simple — your commander is a two-cost mana dork that ramps you to seven mana by turn four. A number of content creators have picked the idea up recently, but as far as I can tell the original source is [Salubrious Snail's 2024 video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ceILMLrNCGw), which is where I first found it.

{% include card-grid.html cards="Wandertale Mentor" %}

My version of this concept in PDH is helmed by [[Wandertale Mentor]], a two-mana Gruul dork with an expend trigger that synergizes perfectly with our high-cost gameplan, growing with +1/+1 counters each turn of the game. It's not uncommon that this "friendly" raccoon will eliminate opponents through commander damage alone. It's also my most budget deck, costing me less than $10 at the time I built it, though a handful of the cards have grown in value since.

I went back and forth on whether to label this a cPDH guide. In 2025, across 18 competitive games, the deck posted a [~35% win rate](https://app.cpdh.guide/year-1-results). In 2026 so far, it's cooled off, sitting at 25% across 16 games — still average for a competitive deck. I've decided to call it a non-cPDH guide to keep our labeling conservative.

*Editor's note: the deck has played more games since this article was written, with the 2026 win rate fluctuating as a result. It currently sits at an 18.2% winrate across 22 matches.*

The real strength of this deck is how downright simple and straightforward it is. It's my favorite deck to introduce players to PDH with. There are no tricks, and the gameplan is telegraphed - there's no infinites, just raw power. It has found the most competitive success in pods with multiple combo players, where opponents pose risks of winning through infinites — often making a meme-worthy [[Colossal Dreadmaw]] an unworthy target for countermagic and interaction.

At the end of this article, I explore alternate commanders for this deck as well as commanders for the same strategy in different colors. If you do choose to build this type of ramp deck for yourself, I implore you to try going all in on the 2-4-7 strategy, even if you're skeptical. In the deck we're looking at today, my curve is strict. There are only four cards that cost less than 4 mana. That's it. I only run them because those cards happen to be extremely impactful on the game — there is no better haste enabler than [[Tuktuk Rubblefort]]. I've seen lists that slot too many two- and three-cost spells, and none of those lists has seen meaningful success in competitive settings. To each their own, but I solemnly believe making each card in your deck extremely high-value provides overwhelming value that can be difficult to handle.

This is the PDH deck that brings me the most joy, and I'm delighted to share this joy with you. Go forth, build your own ramping, stomping, winning list, and I hope you enjoy it as much as I've enjoyed my own Trash Panda and his humongous friends!

## Gameplan Summary

Most of my deck techs open by naming a precise win condition — a specific combo, a storm line, something that closes the game with certainty. This deck doesn't work that way.

Instead of focusing on how we close the game, this deck is focused on how we open: curve out perfectly through the early turns to hit six or seven mana by turn four, then slam an oversized creature every single turn thereafter. My big dinosaurs go "Rawr!" and that's the whole plan. The win comes from large increments of accumulated stats and bodies, building your board presence until it's too big to be handled.

It plays, in spirit, like a traditional aggro deck from 60-card Magic: the curve is tight, the sequencing matters, and the deck rewards precise, disciplined play.

## The Early Turns

The deck lives and dies by its opening four turns. Here's the ideal sequence:

1. **Turn one — the only flexible turn.** Use your mana to cycle an [[Oliphaunt]], [[Generous Ent]], or [[Ash Barrens]]; crack a fetch for a basic; or simply play a tapped land.
2. **Turn two — deploy the commander.** Play a land that provides the other color of Wandertale Mentor's Gruul cost, then cast your commander.
3. **Turn three — ramp hard.** With your third land down and the commander tapping for mana, you have four mana to spend on a dedicated mana accelerant. The deck runs a healthy package of four-mana ramp spells that net you two additional mana, putting you at six mana going into turn four.
4. **Turn four — start the beatings.** You now have six mana — seven if you make your fourth land drop. From here, the game is simply a matter of dropping the most expensive, most punishing creatures you can cast, turn after turn after turn.

That's all, folks! Hit the curve, present threats early, and don't let up on the gas.

## Mulligans

Now that we know what our early turns are going to look like, we need to learn how to mulligan. If you're not used to mulliganing low, there is no better deck in PDH to learn these principles. You can comfortably mulligan down to four cards at the start of the game, so long as you have the right four cards. In this case, the right four cards are:

- Three lands — one of them can be a tapped or fetch land to play on turn one, but you need at least two of your three lands to be untapped to stay on curve. Similarly, you need to make sure your lands will be able to provide both red and green mana on turn two to put Wandertale into play.
- One of our four-mana acceleration pieces.

It's much better to have your early curve correct so you can play any stompy creature you draw, rather than trying to keep an unideal hand without your four-mana ramp spells — forcing you to play your big creatures on turn five or six.

I'm currently running 12 of these four-mana ramp spells, [[Writhing Chrysalis]] being a recent inclusion to help find good mulligans more consistently.

{% include card-grid.html cards="Stone-Seeder Hierophant, Krosan Drover, Sisay's Ring, Market Festival, Dawn's Reflection, Ranger's Path, Skyshroud Claim, Ur-Golem's Eye, Firemind Vessel, Oasis Ritualist" %}

If you truly hate the idea of mulliganing so low, you can also run more cards that provide temporary mana spikes to help you hit the four-mana acceleration more consistently, though your late game may suffer, as you'll still be reliant on lands to play your 8-drops.

## Card Selection

The nice thing about this deck is that you can fill it out with your favorite selection of big, burly beaters. Raid your bulk box and play all those mana-intensive creatures you never had a home for. My recommendation in your search: avoid creatures with less than four toughness. Four toughness is the threshold to avoid dying to almost all board wipes in the format ([[Pestilence]] or [[Crypt Rats]] being the exceptions) and a strong majority of damage-based removal. In addition, it's been my experience that three keywords will contribute significantly to your success: vigilance, trample, and cascade.

## *Vigilance*

Vigilance is probably the most important keyword in the deck, so I make it my top priority. Since the deck wants to swing out and beat faces in, it's easy to leave ourselves open and vulnerable to crack-back attacks. Vigilance allows us to push damage while also keeping up blockers to protect ourselves. You'll also notice that I've kept a [[Haunted Cloak]] in my list — being able to swing with the creature you just played and keep it up as a blocker, maximizing your damage each turn? It's just *chef's kiss.*

{% include card-grid.html cards="Oakgnarl Warrior, Iron Giant, Beacon Behemoth, Elfhame Wurm, Skysnare Spider" %}

## *Trample*

Trample isn't something you really need to try for. You're running Gruul cards, and the best ones you can find — you'll be tripping over all of your trample cards. This is our deck's version of "evasion" to keep pushing damage past blockers. The ones that say "creatures you control have trample" are the big hitters you can't miss! Unfortunately, there are only two that I think are super solid: [[Nylea's Forerunner]] and [[Spectral Hunt-Caller]]. There are a few that provide the same effect on ETB or when they attack, but the timing can be off, and I'd rather not risk it. I also ran [[Summon: Fat Chocobo]] for some time, but the fact that it sacrifices itself at the end of the Saga made it detrimental when I had to continue into the long game.

{% include card-grid.html cards="Colossal Dreadmask, Molten Monstrosity, Wrecking Beast, Nylea's Forerunner, Havenwood Wurm" %}

## *Cascade*

If you watched the Salubrious Snail video linked at the top of this article, you'll already know that a big motivator in his deck design was maximizing the impact of the free spells you find off cascade triggers. I've kept the same principle alive in my version of the deck, only allowing four cards into my build that are under four mana — they were too good to give up. You'll notice that I don't run [[Natural Reclamation]]. It hits ramp such a high percentage of the time that it's not actually helping the deck progress in the mid- to late-game. I also only run one card at a time in the deck with a mana value of two or lower, so I can turn [[Violent Outburst]] into an instant-speed tutor for that card. I'm currently running [[Flaring Pain]] as a way to get through my opponents' fog effects, but I've recently tried to find another card that's more impactful on average — I'm considering [[Moment's Peace]], as it also benefits from flashback, making it difficult to counter.

{% include card-grid.html cards="Violent Outburst, Annoyed Altisaur, Maelstrom Colossus, Boarding Party" %}

## *Other Notable Cards*

There are a few cards that give us double strike, several with effective discard effects — my favorite being [[Zhur-Taa Swine]] — a sprinkling of graveyard effects in [[Bramble Wurm]] and [[Fin-Clade Fugitives]], and, of course, the big choomba himself, [[Ulamog's Crusher]]. They really don't make big, expensive common creatures like they used to.

{% include card-grid.html cards="Ulamog's Crusher" %}

Adventure creatures add a little extra utility with their cheaper spells attached; don't be scared to sac lands to [[Seismic Monstrosaur]] when your hand is low; and [[Fangren Marauder]] is an effective counter to [[Malcolm, Keen-Eyed Navigator]] combos, while also being fantastic in a format where Treasures, Clues, and [[Lotus Petals]] are being sacrificed regularly.

## Interaction Strategy

What is *interaction*? We don't have that 'round these parts.

In all seriousness, the deck wants to interact only with our opponents' faces, killing them fast. I run some incidental removal — like [[Skoa, Embermage]], which comes attached to a decent body and gets our expend trigger — but I do not make it a priority in any way.

In the words of MtG writer and "Beatdown King" Dave Price: "There are wrong answers, but there are never wrong threats." We are coming to the battlefield with threats. Let your opponents worry about the answers.

## Playing Against the Deck

If you're sitting across from Wandertale Mentor and want to slow it down, a core weakness lies in its tight curve. The cleanest answer is to bolt the bard: kill the commander on turn two, before it can tap for the mana that fuels the turn-three accelerator. Do that, and you don't stop the gameplan outright — the same sequence will still unfold — but you push the clock back by a turn. Against a deck this dependent on precise tempo, buying yourself that extra turn can be the difference between getting run over and stabilizing. It further makes the Wandertale player reliant on getting their mana through lands to execute their gameplan.

However, don't bolt the bard just because you can. If you have a Malcolm player and a Wandertale player at the table, the turbo combo deck should be your first priority. Use sound threat assessment before making decisions in all your games.

## Variations on a Theme

There are several commanders that you can slot in place of Wandertale Mentor, all equally effective at providing mana in a timely manner but none of which match Wandertale's ability to scale in size over the course of the game. I myself keep [[Ruby, Daring Tracker]] in my deckbox to avoid needing to Rule Zero Wandertale into EDH games. If you choose to run more instants in your build, [[Radha, Heir to Keld]] will provide more value by giving you two extra mana a turn when she attacks.

{% include card-grid.html cards="Ruby Daring Tracker, Radha Heir to Keld, Kessig Naturalist" %}

You can also find compatible commanders for the same style of play outside of Gruul. [[Skull Prophet]] gives you the ability to mill cards in Golgari when you're not using it for mana. [[Rootcoil Creeper]] is a Simic variant which provides more mana for spells cast from your graveyard, while also being able to retrieve flashback cards from exile. Think of the value! [[Hermetic Herbalist]] is another recent Simic inclusion to this list, providing extra mana for Lesson spells. If mono-colored decks are more your speed, [[Animal Attendant]] is also an excellent option, as it buffs almost all of the creatures you would want to be running with +1/+1 counters. It's like Wandertale, but the buffs spread across your board instead of staying on your commander.

In the process of writing this article, I discovered what I thought did not exist: a Selesnya version of this type of commander! But it's here, it's real, it's [[Murasa Rootgrazer]]! She even offers BETTER ramp than Wandertale does! You're learning this with me, folks. Fresh off the press.

{% include card-grid.html cards="Skull Prophet, Rootcoil Creeper, Hermitic Herbalist, Murasa Rootgrazer, Animal Attendant" %}

Wandertale Mentor and Animal Attendant are probably the strongest of the options I've provided here, because of their abilities to provide lasting creature buffs into the late game. Perhaps someone will find a way to take the Rootcoil Creeper or Skull Prophet to new heights using their abilities, but until then I stake  my claim that these two are the best 2-cost mana dorks to have in your PDH command zone.

Another commander which maintains the essence of our strategy while shifting the entire pacing of the deck — and might be better than the two-cost commanders — is [[Elves of Deep Shadow]].

{% include card-grid.html cards="Elves of Deep Shadow" %}

Elves of Deep Shadow shifts the strategy from 2-4-7 to 1-3-5, starting your acceleration a turn earlier and landing your first big creature on turn three instead of turn four. Your ramp spells would cost three instead of four for this build, reducing the amount of mana you accelerate by, but also improving mulligans in hands where you have [[Cultivate]] or [[Kodama's Reach]], as they will put your third land drop right into your hand. You also wouldn't be able to run any tapped or fetch lands in this deck, because you have to be even more perfect in your curve starting from turn one.

I have not yet built or experimented with [[Elves of Deep Shadow]], so eager brewers — show us what you've got!
