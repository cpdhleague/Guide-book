I get a particular delight watching competitors at cPDH tournaments place, or win, with unexpected new brews. That's exactly what happened this past weekend at the [Cloudy Commons Petal-Palooza](https://topdeck.gg/event/cloudy-commons-cup-iv) on July 19th in Minneapolis, held in partnership with North Star cEDH and offering a Masterpiece Lotus Petal as the first-place prize. Ken Cockriel, piloting [[Hawkeye, Young Avenger]] from the new Marvel Super Heroes set, came out on top to claim that beautiful card. When I congratulated him, the first thing he said was, "It was rather unexpected, actually."

{% include card-grid.html cards="Hawkeye Young Avenger" %}

## Why Hawkeye?

I asked Ken what drew him to the new mono-red commander, and why he thought it was ready for heated competition. "I just saw a card I wanted to play and I built it the way that I thought it could be built."

Ken elaborated: "I'm a red player. I've always been a red player. Legacy Burn, Modern Burn, Goblins and EDH, whatever. I'm just drawn to red." Because he pays attention to red, Hawkeye stuck in his mind. "The wheels started turning, and I'm like, this is a good card. You learn to recognize when the cards are good, right?"

Later, sorting through old cards and taking decks apart, he ran into a Rakdos Sacrifice list from Standard a few years back. In the sideboard was End the Festivities. "As I'm reading [[End the Festivities]], it made me think of Hawkeye and I'm like, wait a minute, this might just give Hawkeye the best board wipes in the format… The format really only has [[Crypt Rats]] that can deal with anything bigger than two toughness." With Hawkeye buffing his non-combat damage, all those little "deal 1 to each" effects suddenly wipe most boards in the format. "If Hawkeye is on the board, [End the Festivities] lightning bolts everything."

{% include card-grid.html cards="End the Festivities, Tectonic Hazard, Scouring Sands, Electrickery" %}

Mixed in with his board wipes are many pingers, some of our format's strongest cards, slowly deteriorating opponents' life totals — unless those pings resolve with Hawkeye on the battlefield, turning a constant burn into a searing win attempt through non-combat damage.

Another keen insight came from Ken's discussion of Hawkeye's timing. It's not always correct to jam her out on turn three or four. "It's kind of a table read," he explained. "If you're not experiencing interaction, then you can absolutely jam her out on turn four and probably win on turn five." But running her into open mana is usually a good way to watch her die. So Ken played patiently through many of his matches, developing a board of cheap pingers that quietly pressure the table. "You can wait for your opening to drop Hawkeye and just win the game on the spot."

## Tournament Day, Round by Round

**Round one** was the nightmare pairing on paper. Ken's biggest fear with a glass-cannon red deck is removal, "and the best removal in the game is in black. And I didn't want to see any black across the table from me." He sat down into an all-black pod. But it oddly favored him: one player got stuck on two lands, and Seat 1 became fixated on repeatedly killing [[Whiplash, Vengeful Engineer]] in Seat 4. That let Ken set up "relatively scot-free and just jam Hawkeye on turn 4 and untap and win the game on turn 5." (Not one of his patient matches.) The [[Downhill Charge]] that sealed it was a late addition suggested by North Star cEDH organizer Andrew — "and that card came through in spades."

**Round two** got grindy and a little more tense. It came down to Hawkeye and a Gut player: Ken at eight life, facing a developed [[Gut, True Soul Zealot]] board. He had to decide whether to risk an instant-speed four damage by not blocking, knowing blocking would cost him the win the following turn. Gut did have a burst of damage, but it only gave +2 power, leaving Ken alive at two. He took the hit, untapped, and burned out the table.

**Round three** Ken lost. "That should have been a draw, but I was cocky." In a pod with three blue decks, he played "the grindiest game maybe I've ever played in my life," never even casting Hawkeye. He offered a draw early and was refused. Later, when a draw was offered back to him, he was at 14 life with two players nearly dead on board — so he declined, reasonably certain he was winning. Then the [[Third Path Iconoclast]] player dealt 14 sudden damage to take him out, finishing the other two in the same turn. "Remember this," Ken told me, "because we have to come back to this."

**Round four** was a win largely off an opponent's misplay: another player countered the [[Goreclaw, Terror of Qal Sisma]] player's [[Ulamog's Crusher]] while knowing Ken was about to deploy his commander. "That doesn't even get to attack until the next turn," I said. "Correct," Ken replied. He untapped, deployed Hawkeye behind a [[Pyroblast]], and killed the table.

## The Final Match

The finals put Ken across from two players who had already seen his deck — including the TPI pilot, John, who'd knocked him out in round three. "Now they know [my] game plan," Ken said. "When two of the three people know what I'm doing when I sit down, I no longer have that advantage. And that's kind of a problem."

But surprise cuts both ways — Ken had also learned what *they* were doing. The pod was a [[Sanar, Unfinished Genius]] control deck, a Weavers deck, and John on TPI. Both control decks mulliganed; both aggro decks kept their second sevens. "The two aggro decks were on more cards than the control decks, which I think is where we want to be as aggro decks."

{% include card-grid.html cards="Ley Weaver, Lore Weaver, Hawkeye Young Avenger, Sanar Unfinished Genius, Third Path Iconoclast" %}

What followed was a nearly two-hour grind. Ken established two pingers early — a [[Kessig Flamebreather]] and a wizard token off [[Mysidian Elder]] — and chipped away while his opponents played the control game. He had a [[Rubble Rouser]] out, "actually kind of the perfect card for this deck," since it makes mana from the graveyard and pings each opponent when it does. Around turn seven or eight he tried to jam Hawkeye — partly, he admitted with amusing honesty, because "this is my second time on camera against these people, and I haven't played Hawkeye yet. I don't want to look bad on camera." She got countered. But it left his opponents tapped out, and he popped a [[Tectonic Hazard]] that set the TPI player back six or seven tokens.

{% include card-grid.html cards="Kessig Flamebreather, Mysidian Elder, Rubble Rouser, Thermo-Alchemist" %}

The game turned on a counter war Ken had nothing to do with. The Weavers pilot tried to resolve a [[Freed from the Real]]; everyone else tapped out fighting over it. The Freed didn't land, but no one had mana left either. "So my turn, I have enough mana to play Hawkeye. I killed Weavers and Sanar off and left TPI at one life."

And then John made what Ken called "the most heroic effort I have ever seen a Magic player make to try to win a game." With his TPI, an [[Ingenious Artillerist]], and a [[Guttersnipe]] out, every spell became a fistful of damage. He chained spells, made tokens, and dealt Ken *twenty damage* in a single turn, dragging him from 24 life down to four. But it wasn't quite enough.

"I was just able to untap the [[Thermo-Alchemist]] to finish it off."

## Deck Specs

Having seen others theory-craft Hawkeye, I was curious about some of Ken's inclusions and exclusions. Inspired to build her only a short time before the tournament, he admitted the deck might need tweaking to reach its fully optimized build. I suggested he might be running too many creatures; he agreed, and spoke about running more board wipes as he discovers them.

He explained why [[Lightning Bolt]] is a bad card here (sorry Alkadron, second time a mono-red player is gonna shock you with this opinion!). "How do you put in a Lightning Bolt when you're cutting End the Festivities or a Tectonic Hazard, which is multiple Lightning Bolts for the same price? It becomes an act of favoritism."

I was also delighted by some of Ken's card choices — cards I'd never noticed before. "[[Hissing Iguanar]] is my [[Blood Artist]]. It's the half of a Blood Artist that red cares about." He prototyped the deck on Arena before putting it to paper, and Hissing Iguanar isn't on Arena. "If you curve Hissing Iguanar into Hawkeye, and she survives the turn, and you get to board wipe the next turn, the game probably ends." Even more of a bummer, he didn't see Iguanar on tournament day either.

{% include card-grid.html cards="Hissing Iguanar, Silver Deputy, Sunscorched Desert" %}

He also shared his dream of one day running a [[Hawkeye's Bow]] mana-dork chain. "It's the magical Christmas land with the mana dorks and the bow," he said, before laying out the sequence — equip the Bow to an [[Iron Myr]], tap for red and deal damage, use that mana to move it to an [[Ornithopter of Paradise]], tap and deal again, move it to a Rubble Rouser (which pings twice off its own ability), and finally to [[Krark-Clan Stoker]] to sacrifice the Bow for two mana and one last ping. "[The idea] made me laugh, and I didn't get to do it, so I was a little bit sad."

{% include card-grid.html cards="Hawkeye's Bow, Iron Myr, Ornithopter of Paradise, Krark-Clan Stoker" %}

He built it this time with an emphasis on baubles. His advice for future brewers? "It was fun. I enjoyed playing it. I liked the deck. I just think it's one of the worst ways to build Hawkeye."

## On the Meta

As always, I close my interviews by asking the winner their thoughts on the meta. "I think the top 10 [in the tournament] only had two decks that weren't blue. It was me and Whiplash." Beyond the prevalence of blue, Ken observed, "I don't think there's anything unbeatable in the meta right now. If there's only two decks in the meta, then there's a problem. And that doesn't feel like the case here."

He's enjoying brewing with 'unintentional commanders,' the non-legendary uncommons, pointing to [[Flux Channeler]]: "You can't play that as your commander in regular EDH, and there's not a Legendary Creature yet that has that text on it. So that's a commander you can only play in PDH, and that's really cool."

He also shared that he's got a [[Speedball, New Warrior]] deck on the shelf waiting to be revisited, but the yet-unreleased [[Tinybones, Pocket Nuisance]] will likely be his next brew.

{% include card-grid.html cards="Flux Channeler, Speedball New Warrior, Tinybones Pocket Nuisance" %}

## Closing Comment

On the impact of his win: "I would not call this anything but a rogue deck. I don't expect to see Hawkeye decks popping up all over the place."

I hope Ken is wrong. I think this commander has proven its potential in the hands of a patient and thoughtful pilot, and I hope we see more of her.

Congrats again, Ken!
